Annotated Bibliography

Green, Fiona. "How and Why Wildfires Are Becoming More Common: Looking at the Past 10 Years of Wildfire Seasons in North America." Clarity Movement, 25 June 2025, www.clarity.io/blog/how-and-why-wildfires-are-becoming-more-common-looking-at-the-past-10-years-of-wildfire-seasons-in-north-america.

This article addresses the factors leading to the increasingly severe wildfire seasons that are occurring across North America over the past decade. Key points of interest include climate change, human activity, and the continuous encroaching of urban environments in nature. This is quite relevant to my project’s dystopian narrative, as it provides solid data on the extent of destruction that is occurring, once disastrous is now considered routine. A very interesting detail mentioned in the article are all of the toxic pollutants that have been released into the air as a direct consequence of wild fires burning through human settlements, which are significantly more hazardous than what simple vegetation fires are capable of. The publisher of the article is Clarity Movement, which is an air quality monitoring company with reasonable credibility. The article concludes by noting that the continued worsening of greenhouse emissions would only exacerbate and prolong the presence of costly and destructive fires for the foreseeable future. As a resident of California, the topic of wild fires have been all too relevant, especially within the past decade. Things are truly going to get worse if we as a society don’t start doing something about this mess.

Hultgren, Andrew, et al. "Climate Change Cuts Global Crop Yields, Even When Farmers Adapt." Stanford Doerr School of Sustainability, 18 June 2025, sustainability.stanford.edu/news/climate-change-cuts-global-crop-yields-even-when-farmers-adapt.

This study offers a very comprehensive look into how Global Warming will affect the yield of staple food crops like wheat, rice, corn, and soy. It provides a connection to one of the main narratives of the project. There is an estimate that at 3 degrees celsius of warming, global food production could face reduction by the equivalent of 1 meal for every person on Earth. A key detail of this study is how it accounted for adaptation on the part of the farmers themselves, where they concluded that even the most optimistic adjustments made can only offset about ⅓ of potential losses. Andrew Hultgren, a lead researcher from the University of Illinois states that by 2100, global crop yields could drop by almost a quarter. This study is by and far one of the most alarming things I’ve read during this research session, simply due to the implications of the consequences, and the inevitable suffering that will stem from our inaction.

“Extreme Heat and Climate Change.” Center for Climate and Energy Solutions, 4 Feb. 2026, www.c2es.org/content/heat-waves-and-climate-change/.

This site is from the Center for Climate and Energy Solutions, a nonprofit, nonpartisan organization that has close connections to the federal climate policy. It provides an overview of the way rising temperatures are influencing agriculture, health, and infrastructure in America. It is relevant to the capstone project as it presents statistics such as the dramatic increase in the occurrence of extreme heat events compared to decades before. A notable feature is its documentation of how the increase in heat worsens other relevant issues, such as droughts, wildfires, and worsening air quality. The issue of extreme heat is another prominent issue that is directly felt by most people today, especially with heat records being broken year after year. It is quite scary to think about just how much worse things are going to get until the decision makers wake up to the reality that we are in.
